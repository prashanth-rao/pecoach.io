Have you ever wondered what makes you hungry?

Why does dessert after a big meal still look appealing, even when you're stuffed?

Why might you get hungry when you're sort of stressed (like a bad day at work), but not hungry when you're very stressed (like running for your life)?

The answer is both chemical and mechanical: a complex play of communicating chemicals known as hormones and cytokines (cell signals), and mechanical signals from the digestive tract.

Hunger is a symphony of hormonal and mechanical interaction, driven by the master conductor—the brain.

The brain is a food finder

Because humans have mostly evolved in environments where food was scarce, the body's sense organs and their processors in the brain are exquisitely attuned to the project of finding and consuming food.

Seeing food

Having eyes in the front of our heads, like many other high-food-chain predators, lets us easily spot and chase food sources.

That visual processing loves bright colors in particular—like tasty fruit!

Similarly, this also means we're very susceptible to processed foods that use bright colors, shiny packaging, and appealing food advertisements.

Smelling food

The nose can detect thousands of scents at very low concentrations. This lets us sniff out (and avoid) bad stuff, as well as go towards good stuff.

In fact, you need to smell your food in order to taste it. If food doesn’t give retronasal passages something to sniff, you'll tend to feel less satiated from food. This is one way that eating slowly is physiologically more satisfying.

Digesting food

The mouth and tongue can detect and sort out a vast range of tastes and textures; saliva secretes enzymes that start to break down food before we even make contact with the food.

We eat with the brain before food touches the lips.

Eating whole foods slowly and mindfully is how these systems work best. Those systems are fully active and cooperative when they're supported with regular movement.

Alternatively, processed foods are engineered in ways that take advantage of these systems and hijack mindful eating. Many processed foods are hyperpalatable (nearly irresistible) to the seeing, smelling and digesting body mechanisms.

When there's abundant food around, especially hyperpalatable food, natural systems can tend to overreact or malfunction.

The digestive tract

Every square inch of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract is devoted to sensing and processing food.

The gut is so dense with sensory mechanics that it's called the "second brain", the enteric nervous system. This nervous system operates on the same chemical software (neurotransmitters) as the upstairs brain.

Body awareness of hunger

Developing body awareness and sensitivity means practicing sorting out hunger signals from other emotional signals. For example, being more body aware, you could more clearly discriminate between hunger and boredom. If you're eating for hunger rather than emotional distress, it's easier to feel more satisfied on less food.

You can learn to know and trust hunger signals by practicing noticing and body awareness.

Hunger in-tune with the environment

Hundreds of thousands of years of evolution have given humans amazingly powerful and productive internal regulation systems.

More natural environments and foods support healthy regulation systems. More processed and artificial environments and foods can disrupt natural regulation systems. External foods and environments that compete with internal regulation make staying healthing feel like a struggle.

That's stressful. And recurring stress on your body will continue to affect a more naturally balanced hunger system.
