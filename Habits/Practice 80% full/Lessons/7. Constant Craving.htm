Learn where those cravings come from and how to bust them.

Let's start with understanding the science of cravings and how those "gut feelings" about food (aka your enteric nervous system) work.

"Want to eat" vs. "need to eat"

It's easy to confuse emotions or other physical sensations (such as anxiety) with hunger.

Cravings often feel different from more whole-body hunger.

Where do cravings come from?

The brain is a busy, complicated place.

It has to coordinate input from many sources in the body: body fat, the gastrointestinal tract, the spinal cord and other body tissues.

What do you want food to do for you?

Fundamentally, a craving is a strong belief that eating a particular food will ease an unwanted emotion.

We expect a particular food to do something meaningful for us—to somehow change what we're experiencing right now.

That makes sense. Food elicits powerful chemical signals in the body. Even the thought of some foods can start a cascade of feel-good signaling. That's especially true for emotionally charged food, the food of cravings.

The feel-good neurotransmitters: serotonin and dopamine

When we eat, (and sometimes before we eat) we release serotonin and dopamine. Serotonin and dopamine are neurotransmitters.

Serotonin makes us feel chilled and relaxed. It also tells us to stop eating.

Dopamine, in contrast, is our "reward" neurotransmitter. It gives us a "high". Dopamine is the chemical that encourages us to seek out the "hit" of a brief and intense thrill.

In other words, serotonin is the "aaahhh…" chemical, while dopamine is the "woowee!!" chemical.

Eating as self-medication

Eating can be a kind of "self-medication" that helps calm or energize a mood. Bodies are biologically wired to respond that way.

For example, newborn babies can be soothed by sucking on things, or given sugar solutions as a painkiller. The movement of the jaw stimulates a nerve that helps release serotonin.

That's biology. People aren't "bad" for looking to soothe with food. It makes sense.

This brings up an important caution. Food manufacturers understand neurobiology and psychology, too. Those biological signals are often crafted into marketing tricks to help sell.

Marketing can target the more vulnerable food cravings, spotlighting foods that are hyperpalatable:

  • sugary
  • fatty and creamy
  • salty
  • all of the above, simultaneously
  • varied in texture (e.g., crunchy, creamy, and chewy all together)
  • bright, attractive, and smell good

That sums up most processed convenience foods. Such foods can become "drugs" of choice, because (exactly like other drugs) they make us feel better emotionally, at least temporarily.

Reduce cravings with smart food choices

The good news is that we can reduce cravings with smart food choices.

Whole foods nourish us but don't give us the intense "hit" of processed foods.

Serotonin and dopamine also depend to some degree on protein, fat, and micronutrient levels. If we eat plenty of protein and healthy fats along with a wide range of vitamins and minerals from whole foods, brains stay happy and less vulnerable to cravings.

Also, more time spent chewing (like when we eat whole foods slowly), will often mean feeling more physically satisfied.

Do I crave stuff I need?

While head hunger and cravings are emotionally charged, they also give clues to deeper more physiological needs.

For instance, chocolate is a common craving. One clue within this craving could possibly be magnesium. Chocolate contains magnesium and magnesium is a common nutrient deficiency.

It's common that cravings aren’t directed toward the healthy, nutrient-rich foods that actually contain all those vitamins and minerals that could be lacking. We don't typically crave spinach, broccoli, or lean red meat.

We usually crave nutrient-empty foods, especially when those foods are marketed for their hyperpalatability, and have emotionally charged imprints in our brains. For example, high-sugar milk chocolate from popular brands that inspire nostalgia of childhood treats.

Craving-busters

Here are some tips to help deal with cravings.

  • Understand that cravings are normal. They come and go.
  • If the craving is minor, ignore it.
  • If the craving is moderate, distract yourself.
  • If the craving is overwhelming, have a small portion of what you're craving in a safe environment, such as a restaurant where you can only get one portion of the food (instead of the whole thing). Eat it slowly. Stay checked in to the fullness signals from your body.
  • Keep a "craving diary". Write down what you're craving and when. Also write down what you're thinking and feeling at that time. Over time, look for patterns. Once you know the pattern, you can disrupt it.
  • For women, keep track of your menstrual cycle on a calendar. Two weeks before your period is due can be an especially vulnerable time of cravings.
  • Substitute something that gives you the same feeling. For instance, munch on raw veggies when you're craving crunchiness. Take a hot bath when you're craving warmth and comfort. Find recovery strategies that work for you in a variety of situations.
  • Go for a brisk walk when you're craving distraction. Chew sugarless gum, nibble celery, or drink water if you're craving something to do with your mouth.
  • If you're having constant, powerful cravings, that may be a sign of a hormonal imbalance or nutrient deficiency. Consider talking to your health care provider and getting tested.
