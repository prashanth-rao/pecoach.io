If done smart, fasting is a self-experiment with high rewards.

The instructions of this self-experiment are simple.

For one day, don't eat anything.

Focus on learning

The goal of dietary experiment days is to learn. To learn about:

  • your body
  • your habits
  • your needs
  • your likes and dislikes

At the end of today's experiment, ask yourself:

  • What was this experience like for me?
  • What did this experience teach me about myself and what works for me?

This experiment isn't meant to convince you that a certain way of eating is "bad" or "good". It's to focus in on what you learn about yourself.

Why fast?

Many people who’ve tried it say that a fasting day is one of the hardest—and most illuminating—experiences they've ever had.

You learn what true hunger feels like.

Many of us no longer recognize the difference between psychological hunger (i.e., wanting to eat) and physiological hunger (i.e., needing to eat).

The only way to really experience true physiological hunger—to tease it out from the background noise—is to stop eating for an extended period.

You face your fear—and come out swinging.

Are you afraid of being hungry? Many people are.

Fasting for a day, you'll face that fear. You'll learn what it feels like when you're legitimately hungry, and how strong intentions can drive behavior above body impulses to eat.

Taking the risk of purposely being hungry can teach you about the power of a strong commitment amid stress.

It's an incredible feeling (and often a great relief) when you learn that you can feel hungry—truly hungry—and survive.

Assess what's right for you

There are many important reasons to not test this experiment. Fasting can be unhealthy if:

  • You have a medical condition that requires you to eat every day.
  • You struggle with disordered eating and have been told never to fast.
  • You know that periods of food restriction—even if done carefully and consciously—will lead to bingeing later on.
  • You need to be 100% "on your game", such as flying a plane or doing open-heart surgery.

You know your body and life best. Do what you need to do to stay healthy, safe and sane.

Always use outcome-based decision making and your Owner's Manual.

Make this work for you

Pick a day and a schedule that work for you.

You can fast from dinner to dinner. Or maybe you prefer breakfast to breakfast.

If a whole day seems like too much, try just skipping one meal (ideally a meal you normally do eat) and noticing what that's like.

Go hour by hour, if you need to.

Do your best.

It's all a learning experience.

"Will I be OK?”

Fasting is safe for most people.

Under generally good health, going 24 hours without food won't harm you or degrade fitness. Some evidence suggests occasional fasting may offer health benefits.

What to expect

·         Expect to be hungry, of course.

Notice if the experience in the moment matches your expectations. Is it as painful or challenging as you thought it would feel?

·         Expect hunger to come in waves.

Hunger hormones are pulsatile. Hunger feelings will come and go gradually, rather than continually gaining in intensity. For example, hunger could be strongest about 3 to 4-hours after eating, then subside.

·         Expect to notice food a lot more.

Observe how food cues and triggers are all around you, and how you want to respond to them.

·         Expect to confront resistance within yourself.

"You people are crazy!" "I’ll die without lunch!" Practice facing the fear with calm and ease.

·         Expect to progress one moment at a time.

Rather than thinking about the whole 24-hour day, go step by step, minute by minute, moment to moment.

·         Expect this to be a learning experience.

You might not be happy while you're in it. The insight is longer-lasting and invaluable.

·         Expect that you can do this.

Your ancestors thousands of years ago didn’t get three square meals a day. Evolution’s equipped you with the tools to handle this.

At the end of the day...

What do you observe?

When you look back on your day, record your observations in your Owner's Manual.

Expect that you may be hungrier for 1-2 days afterwards.

Be prepared. Plan ahead. Have a "safety plan" to prevent food madness.

And stay "checked in" to whatever you think and feel.
