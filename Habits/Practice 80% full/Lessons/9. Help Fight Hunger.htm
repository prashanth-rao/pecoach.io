Some ideas to help the deeply hungry.

If you've tried extended fasting, you have a sense of what real hunger feels like.

Imagine feeling hunger every day. Rumbling stomach, light-headedness, irritable, anxious... every day. All day. Imagine skipping meals... because you had no choice. Imagine looking at your wallet and realizing you have enough money that day to either pay your rent or feed your kids.

That's the reality faced by millions of low-income people in North America.

Food insecurity

Food insecurity is what happens "at the margins" when people are just scraping by.

They aren't starving per se. Most days they can get enough to eat... sorta. They might have to skip breakfast. Or lunch. Or a day.

Maybe they can put food on the table, but spend a lot of time worrying about how to make that happen. When people with low-income can afford to eat enough, the nutrition is often downgraded with the cost. The most nutritionally bare food, processed and fast, is also the cheapest.

You can eat it... but is it enough?

More and more people are using food banks and the food banks are struggling to meet the need.

One study found that 99% of food hampers provided by food banks did not provide three days worth of nutrients—some only provided little more than a day's worth of energy.

Hampers didn't provide enough:

  • Whole grains and cereals
  • Fruits and vegetables
  • Lean meats and alternatives
  • Vitamins A, B1 (thiamine), B2 (riboflavin), B3 (niacin), B9 (folate), B12, C, and D
  • Minerals, such as calcium, magnesium, iron and zinc

What you can do

Here are some ideas from small to big.

  • Be a good neighbor. If you know your kid's friend isn't getting much healthy stuff at home, you could quietly put out a few extra healthy snacks next time he visits.
  • If you contribute non-perishable foods to a food drive, make them nutrient-rich options, e.g., canned veggies, lentils, etc.
  • Share tips on how to prepare healthy but inexpensive foods, such as canned beans or in-season greens.
  • Donate to a local food agency or food bank, but ask that they use your donation to purchase healthy foods. Many food banks can, in fact, buy and store healthy perishables like vegetables. Instead of dropping off a couple of boxes of macaroni 'n' cheese, see if you can help your local community food agencies buy more healthy stuff.
  • Organize a healthy potluck to raise awareness and food agency funds.
  • Share the excess produce from your backyard garden or fruit tree.
  • Get involved in a community vegetable garden project.
  • Volunteer at a food agency in your community.
  • Help organize or participate in a healthy food drive—perhaps as part of a community organization you already belong to, such as a church, mosque, or synagogue.
  • Help organize or participate in a school breakfast or lunch program.
