Imagine that you’ve just finished a tough workout. Your legs feel like jelly from squats and you can barely lift your arms thanks to the pull-ups. You sweated hard enough to drench a t-shirt.

Why “working it off” doesn't work

Rachel is a 30-year-old, 5’5", 150 lb woman.

Rachel’s basal metabolic rate (BMR) is 1472 calories per day. BMR is the energy required just to stay alive: breathing, digestion, etc. That means she’s expending about 61 calories an hour simply lying in bed.

If Rachel goes out and jogs for an hour, she uses 544 calories. So, she’s expended 483 more calories than she would have done just by sleeping. This is a great start.

Then, after her workout, she’s hungry and goes out for lunch. Without a solid understanding of her body’s physiology, the stories running in Rachel’s head are, “I worked out this morning. I earned more food today.”

It’s easy and common for exercise and eating to follow that kind of moral story, one that doesn’t match the way that fat loss actually works.

If Rachel chose a food “reward” of a Big Mac (562 calories) and fries (384 calories). That’s 946 calories… 463 calories more than she just used for exercise.

You do need exercise for leanness, fitness, and a long healthy life. Exercise smart means matching it with more smart and careful food choices, rather than compensating.

For example, mindful eating and eating to 80% full.

Change your behavior, change your body

If this is new information … what can you do about this?

    • Understand your own body and behavior.

    Do you want to eat more after exercise? Or feel like you should? Or that you earned it, regardless of whether you're actually hungry? Maybe like you "deserve a treat" for "doing the chore" of exercise?

    • Change your expectations about what exercise should do for you.

    Exercise won’t erase poor choices, or simply eating more than your body actually needs.

    • Recognize the proper role of exercise in a fat loss program.

    Exercise is not as good as most people think at “burning calories”, particularly for women. Exercise is a lot more like an air traffic controller that tells nutrients where to go and what to do — aka “nutrient partitioning”.

    • Understand that it’s a lot easier to eat a little bit less than to “work it off”.

    • Make nutrition decisions based on your physical cues and evidence, rather than out of fear or because “everyone else does it”.

    You might be worried about running out of energy, or be watching other runners “carb up”. (And if you have a male partner, he might apparently get to eat endlessly.) Follow what your body actually needs, what you know from experiments and observation.

    • Focus on eating to 80% full at each meal, regardless of workouts.

    • Focus on having fun in your workout, rather than making it into a dreadful chore that you get to "compensate" for later.

    If you're really not enjoying the way you’re exercising, find another way to move.
