The 3 Rs

How reframing, refocusing, and resilience equals results.

Good or bad. Rigid or flexible. It's all in how you look at it.

To build the mental skills needed to stick with a practice and reach goals (like living a healthy life), reframing + refocusing + resilience = results.

The mental skills

1. Reframing

2. Refocusing

3. Resilience

These are all mental skills.

Mental skills, like any skill, can be practiced and learned.

You aren't doomed from birth to think or feel a certain way forever, any more than you're doomed from birth never to play the oboe or learn calligraphy.

1. Reframing: Check and set your attitude

Imagine you're a ﬁlm director who's been hired to ﬁlm the movie about your life. Look back on all your experiences and memories through a camera lens. What do you focus on? Whatever you choose to focus on is your frame.

Your frame shapes your attitude and how you interpret your life.

If you use a "negative frame" and shoot your life as a tragedy, that's what it will be. If you use a "positive frame" and shoot your life as an exciting adventure, that's what it will be.

Let's say you have a car accident. You're OK but shaken up, and the car is totaled.

A "negative frame" would shoot this event as "Oh my gawd! How awful! Poor me—my car is trashed!"

A "positive frame" would shoot this event as "Oh my gawd! I am so lucky! Cheated death again! This afﬁrms my commitment to live life to the fullest—as if every moment could be my last! Also, this blood makes me look like a total badass."

Same car accident. Different framing.

In determining what your emotional response to a situation will be, the actual events matter a lot less. Framing matters a lot more.

Here are three quick ways to "reframe" your perspective.

1. Reframe problems as opportunities.
How can you learn and grow from challenges?

2. Reframe "negative" qualities as "superpowers".
Flip the negativity over. If you spend a lot of time daydreaming, maybe your superpower is imagination and creativity.

3. Reframe "I have to…" as "I get to…"
Instead of "I have to go to the gym", try "I get to go to the gym and burn off some stress!"

2. Refocusing: How to get back on track

Your interpretation of your experiences is based on what you pay attention to. And, what you pay attention to will grow.

Learning to deliberately direct your attention is a skill. As all skills, getting good at it takes practice.

Practice developing a re-focusing plan in advance. For example, that could be:

  • A "refocusing ritual". Many athletes have one such as brushing off their hands to symbolize brushing off the last event.
  • A simple verbal trigger that reminds you to pay attention (e.g., "Back on track.").
  • A tried-and-true routine, such as a few familiar go-to meals or strategies.
  • Imagining "parking" your concerns and distractions "outside" while you take care of business in the gym.
  • Getting a quick motivational hit of your "power song" or favorite motivational video or quote.

And remember…

  • You will be distracted. Stuff will happen. That's OK. That's normal.
  • You will need to deal with interruptions, distractions, and an "imperfect" life.
  • There is no such thing as a "losing streak"—every moment is a chance to get back in the game.

Be prepared and have a plan to get back on track.

3. Resilience: Bounce back

All humans are born with a capacity for resilience. Meaning, we have the ability to recover and regroup after a crisis. Humans are driven to survive.

Resilience is a process, rather than a specific action. Resilience is a frame of mind, a way of looking at the world.

To practice resilience, practice the mindset that: No matter what happens to you, you can trust your innate ability to survive... and eventually thrive again.

Mental and physical tension affects resilience.

  • When we're open to learning and change, accept discomfort and newness (even when it's not fair or right), and adjust our balance "on the ﬂy", we keep moving forward.
  • When we're rigid and inﬂexible, fear moving in any direction, and try to control the minutiae of our experiences, we end up exhausted and running—very slowly and awkwardly.
