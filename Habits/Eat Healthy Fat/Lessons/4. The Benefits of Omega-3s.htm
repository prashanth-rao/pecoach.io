What are omega-3 fats?

Omega-3 fats are a type of healthy fat found exclusively in:

  • a few types of nuts and seeds, such as flax seeds, chia seeds, walnuts, and hemp;
  • marine life, such as cold water fish, shellfish, and algae.

Omega-3 fatty acids in fish and algae are what help keep their cold-water bodies from freezing.

Because they stay liquid at low temperatures, omega-3s are like natural antifreeze.

Cool... (literally :)

There are three important forms of omega-3 fats:

  • ALA (alpha-linolenic acid)
  • DHA (docosahexaenoic acid)
  • EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid)

ALA

Most plant-based sources (e.g. flax, walnuts, and chia) are rich in ALA.

EPA/DHA

Marine sources (such as fish and algae) are rich in EPA and DHA.

Our bodies mostly use DHA/EPA, which comes almost exclusively from marine life.

ALA can be converted in the body to DHA/EPA, but most people convert it very inefficiently. So, it’s hard to get enough DHA/EPA from plant sources like flax seeds, and marine sources are essential for balancing fats.

Why are omega-3 fats good?

Omega-3 fats do all kinds of awesome things for the body.

For instance, they can:

  • keep our hearts and brains healthy;
  • lower inflammation;
  • improve our cells' communication;
  • keep joints mobile;
  • help us stay lean; and
  • help build muscle.

There is strong evidence that omega-3s can:

  • improve your blood lipids (like triglycerides and HDL-C);
  • help reduce some symptoms of depression and anxiety;
  • help reduce some ADHD symptoms; and
  • help regulate our metabolism and blood sugar.

And other evidence, though not as strong, for:

  • preventing cancer;
  • preserving memory;
  • improving eye health; and
  • decreasing liver fat.

Fats: Your body’s signal system.

Not all fat we eat gets stored on our bellies and butts.

Our bodies like to use healthy fats to do other important things, like:

  • make fat-based tissues, like our brains and the fatty sheath that insulates our spinal cord and nerves;
  • make many hormones, such as testosterone and estrogen; and
  • make the membranes for all our cells.

The fat we consume becomes an essential part of every one of our cells.

Fat can powerfully influence how our cells communicate and interact.

For example, fat can affect signaling molecules that influence blood vessel constriction, inflammation, blood clotting, pain, airway constriction, etc.

Since our brains are fat-based, changes in fat composition can affect how nerve impulses are transmitted.

This can affect our moods, how well we think, our overall mental health, and even our brain’s longevity.

And, omega-3s may be some of the most powerful and important fats for our bodies.

Should you supplement with omega-3 fats?

The answer to this question depends on you.

If you’re just starting to get the hang of healthy fats:

Keep it simple.

Focus on getting a range of healthy fats from whole foods such as nuts, seeds, avocados, etc.

And if you like fish, try bringing it into your life more regularly: A little sushi here, a little smoked salmon there... it adds up (and tastes good).

If you’re doing those general basics consistently,

Consider adjusting your omega-3 fat intake.

Do you consistently eat fatty cold-water fish like salmon, mackerel, herring, or sardines two to three times per week?

Our guess: If you don’t live in Japan, Crete, Scandinavia, some other coastal fishing village, or up in the Arctic where you’re hunting your own seal and whale to eat, it’s probably a challenge.

If you’re not regularly eating fatty fish, then consider omega-3 supplementation.

Fish oil

Supplement daily with 3–6 grams of total fish oil, giving you around 1–2 grams of total EPA+DHA.

This dose is high enough to give you all the health benefits listed above, while also being very safe.

Krill/algae oil

You can also supplement with krill oil or algae oil, which is plant-based marine life.

With these products, use smaller doses: Aim for 300 mg of total EPA+DHA from these.

While eating plenty of flax seeds, chia seeds, and walnuts, and maybe taking a little flax oil can all be healthy things, they don’t replace eating or supplementing with EPA and DHA from marine life.
