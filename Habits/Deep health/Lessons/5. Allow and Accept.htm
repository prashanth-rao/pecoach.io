Allow and Accept

Allow and accept the presence of unwanted stuff. Then use it to do good.

From "attachment and avoidance" to "allow and accept"

Live in the present, with the body you have. It's the only one you have. It's come a long way, and there's a lot to be proud of.

All anguish comes from attachment.
—Buddhist saying

The only way out is through.
—Carl Jung

Both desire and pain in life comes largely from two things:

  • attachment (to things and ideas) and
  • avoidance (of stuff we don't like or want).

For instance, if you're attached too strongly to ideas like "I must be perfect", "If I'm not lean then I'm unworthy", or "I should have/be ____", then changing your body may not be the source of the pain. It may be that you'll feel crappy even after you're fitter and healthier.

Furthermore, trying to avoid the discomfort of those thoughts or feelings may compound the problem.

There's another way

The opposite of attachment and avoidance: allow and accept.

Allow thoughts and sensations to be present.

Observe whatever comes up. View these thoughts, feelings, and experiences as simply the ebb and flow of existence.

Allow them to move through you, without avoiding them. Without attachment or avoidance, the thoughts can simply flow right through you.

Accept the reality of living, that all kinds of thoughts, feelings, and experiences will constantly come and go.

You don't have to love them. Sit with those thoughts, feelings, and experiences. Rather than avoiding, hang out with it.

Imagine hanging out with your roommate's awkward friend, watching TV on the couch while your roommate gets ready.

The awkward friend is the unwanted thought, feeling or experience. For now, you're stuck with them. So you might as well hang out with them, even if it's uncomfortable.

At the core of this practice is understanding that you have power to change your perceptions.

You are something more than a particular thought, feeling, belief.

Emotions: Your dashboard indicator light

Feelings often seem "irrational". It's easy to feel embarrassed or puzzled by them. When something feels unfamiliar, it's easy to want to make it go away.

However, the human brain produces emotions and physical sensations for a reason: to tell us something important.

Emotions and physical sensations are essential signals that tell us about our environment and what's going on inside us—they're like our dashboard indicator lights.

If we're willing to simply experience our emotions and physical sensations (especially uncomfortable ones), then we might find that it's much easier to tolerate feeling badly.

And if we ask those emotions and physical sensations, "What are you trying to tell me?", then we might gain some critical insights.

You can't control your feelings. You can control your behavior in response to those feelings, and work on grooving new practices in thought and action.
