Motivation That Endures

How to get and stay motivated.

How do you stay motivated for the long haul?

We asked a few coaches who've been training and eating well for many years to share their advice.

10 motivation "secrets"

1. "Discipline" is a myth.

2. Reach out and get support.

3. Take the long view.

4. Consider the alternative.

5. Acknowledge your successes.

6. Do just a little bit more.

7. Change the scenery.

8. Find your "power song".

9. Believe.

10. Live in harmony with your values.

Plus...

A surprise motivation "secret".

"Discipline" is a myth

First, many folks argue that "motivation", "inspiration", or "willpower" aren't what truly drive us.

Strength coach Charles Poliquin:

There is no such thing as discipline. There is only love. Love is the most powerful creative force in the universe.

What do you really love? Because you are the result of what you love most.

You either love finely etched muscular abs more than donuts or you love donuts more than washboard abs you could do your laundry on.

It's as simple as that. Don't beat yourself up because you have no discipline or further drown yourself in a sea of refined carbs.

Admit that you like this food more than you love strength.

One of the best ways to raise self-esteem is to make truly loving choices that lead to increased strength of body and mind.

When you are faced with difficult choices, ask yourself: What would a loving expert recommend?

For example, when choosing desserts, would the loving nutritionist recommend a bowl of berries or the triple decker brownie submerged under melting vanilla ice cream?

When people comment on your results and say things like, "Wow you have a lot of discipline", answer, "No, I just make loving choices for myself."

Reinforcing your own positive behavior will help you grow in strength.

Whenever you make a truly loving choice, say to yourself, "Thank you for taking care of me in a loving way." The more you talk to yourself like a loving parent, the faster you will grow.

Let's say, for example, you just did a single on the squat with a load you didn't feel like doing.

Say, "Wow! I am impressed with your strength of mind! That's why you are a champion!"

Document and reward your successes, and they will grow in magnitude and frequency.

Sustained motivation is a total fantasy for me.

Every day I have to wake up and decide what it's going to be: a day of struggling or a day of rising to the occasion.

Once I decide, then I know what to do. Sometimes, I decide to struggle, and those days are important because I re-learn how much more uncomfortable that is than executing "the grind".

— Coach Cynthia

Reach out and get support

Never underestimate the value of reaching out to your peers or some expert resources for inspiration.

It really doesn't take much. A small token of confidence from someone else can carry you a long way.

Also never underestimate the return you get from helping someone else.

If I really just feel like I can't go on, I’ll let go of what I think I should be doing and go find a way to be focused totally on the welfare of another person. If I spend the day doing that, I'm always refueled to focus on myself by the next day.

— Coach Cynthia

Take the long view

I see 'body transformation' as a lifelong project—there is no end. And it's something I want to keep getting better at with every year, every decade.

— Coach Erin

I enjoy how I live each day. When you enjoy the process, you don’t have to rely so much on motivation.

I eat foods I enjoy and do physical movement that feels good. When I live this way, the world is better off.

I'm more productive in my job/volunteering. I’m a better person to those around me. I feel better. I sleep better. I’m not a burden to health care. Etc.

I remind myself that I have it easy. I can go to the grocery store year round and get any food I want. I can go to a gym 24/7 and do any workout I want. I can turn on my faucet for a clean glass of water. I have a functional body and all the resources I need to succeed.

This stuff never ends. A genuine healthy living project takes a lifetime to complete.

No action is lost. Everything we do matters, even if there is no immediate visible result.

A healthy and lean body is the by-product of healthy and lean living, not an end in itself.

A healthy and lean body is a side effect of how we live.

Value the doing. A lean/healthy body, like most other long-term worthwhile goals, is the sum of countless small, anonymous victories.

— Coach Ryan

Consider the alternative

My motivation is basically this: I'm not going out like a sucker.

I'm going kicking and screaming all the way.

— Coach Krista

As a registered nurse, I've worked with the elderly who find themselves wishing that they could enter a time machine and change their course.

What I do in my 40s, 50s and 60s dictates how I live in my 70s, 80s and 90s.

Will I live in my own home? Make my own meals? Do my own banking? Or will I be dependent on others?

Illnesses such as Type II diabetes and metabolic syndrome are on the rise. The internal destruction these lifestyle illnesses alone can cause is far-reaching.

Now that I've seen this reality, the motivation to stay active long-term is easy. Though there are never guarantees in life, the best defense is a good offence.

Nutrition is something within my control and I take responsibility for this.

— Coach Eileen

Acknowledge your successes

Keep a "daily wins" log. Or at least a mental one. This can dramatically improve your confidence along with your belief in yourself and your ability to achieve your goals.

Over time, you have "real evidence" that you are progressing... and more importantly, have the capacity to progress further.

Humans thrive on positive feedback. It's like our "gasoline". We need to refuel constantly to keep performing well. Every time you acknowledge what you've done well, you "fuel up".

Small daily successes, repeated, create the big ones.

— Coach Kate

Do just a little bit more

Understand that sh*t will happen. There will be "down" days.

On those days, become a minimalist.

Find the smallest thing you can do and do it.

For example, I'll say I’m just going to foam roll and do mobility work. You'll find things will begin to snowball.

After that, I'll start to feel a bit better and say, "I'll just do the first exercise in my program."

From there it may evolve into a complete workout.

If all you ended up doing was your "smallest" thing, then you still can take solace that you did something. If you did more, bonus.

— Coach Jason

It's okay to not feel pumped up all the time. Sometimes it's your body’s way of telling you to slow down or change things up.

I have minimums that I maintain when motivation sinks. I stick to some basics that don't require too much thinking or motivation to do. This keeps me rolling and in the “doing”.

If it's just a day or two, I can move through it easily.

— Coach Erin

Change the scenery

When I'm bored, I change things.

If I'm bored with one activity, I do another. If I'm bored with one food I eat all the time, I substitute another one.

I also like to make up games, challenges, and experiments. I come up with new things to try (e.g., no desserts for X days, no extra food packaging for X days, bodyweight conditioning workouts for 1 month).

I make up game rules or an experimental hypothesis, and then try to play for a while, to see what happens.

— Coach Ryan

Change your focus. If you've been physique focused, find a performance or health goal.

Find something that inspires you and put your attention to that.

Change your environment. New gym or take it outside. If you're tired of cooking, try some healthy meals out. If you’ve been eating out lots, get back in your kitchen. Introduce something new and fun to your "plan".

Change your attitude. Healthy eating/living doesn't come from doing unhealthy things. So stop wishing it could be another way. Decide what you really want and decide if you're willing to do what it takes to get there.

— Coach Erin

Find your “Power Song”

Music is key.

There's always that one song that's guaranteed to fire you up.

I often shuffle or use a random playlist, so I get a selection of new songs for every workout.

Some of these songs are from loooong ago too, and I enjoy the surprises.

Yesterday I almost dropped my dumbbell on my toe when Spice Girls came on. It was totally cool though. Probably made the workout.

— Coach Kate

Believe

There are three important things underlying motivation.

1. You have to want it. Really want it.

2. You have to be willing to work for it, knowing it will be hard and uncomfortable at times. Sometimes really hard.

3. Perhaps the most important: You have to believe you can achieve it. And have strategies in place along with a support system to help continue to remind you of that.

— Coach Kate

Live In harmony with your values

I'm passionate about life. And happiness. I want an abundance of both. I truly enjoy this lifestyle and executing the behaviors. And it allows me to feel and perform at my best.

— Coach Kate

I enjoy the foods I eat and I look forward to moving my body. This is the only way I know how to live now.

— Coach Erin

Be virtuous with exercise and eating. We often let the outcomes dictate our actions. "I'll only exercise and eat right if I lose fat." "I'll only be nice to my spouse when they are nice to me."

We can't do this.

Become unilaterally virtuous. It feels good to be good. Set a personal code and live it.

When you act according to your code, you are rewarded.

— Coach Ryan

The surprise motivation "secret"

Finally, here's some straight talk on motivation and The Grind.

When it comes time to do something—like go to the gym or choose a healthy meal—people might ask themselves:

  • should I go to the gym today?
  • should I eat protein and veggies now?

And they think that when you're 'motivated', the answers are:

  • should I go to the gym today? HELL YEAH! Nothing else I'd rather do!
  • should I eat protein and veggies now? CAN'T WAIT! Bring on the healthy food!

They think motivation is a resounding positive "answer" to the question of whether they should do something good, valuable, necessary, etc.

Of course, this leads to the parallel notion that if there isn’t a resoundingly positive response, they aren't motivated.

And maybe they’re right and that is what motivation means.

The real problem lies in the fact that they think the feeling should drive their action.

In other words, if they feel "hell yes" they think they should act. But if they feel "hell no" they think they should avoid that action.

That's why "motivation" is crap and we need to eradicate the notion that motivation is somehow important to our actions in the world.

Exercise and eating well is ambivalent. Some days you feel like doing it… and you do. Other days you don't feel like doing it… and you don’t.

But you might wonder: If I sometimes feel "hell no"… is there something wrong with me? Of course not. Join the club.

Eventually you must learn to dissociate feeling from action.

You must go to the gym, or eat your vegetables, even if you’re downright screaming "AW HELL NO!!" like Chris Rock.

For the first little while, this is confusing. Dissociating a long-held belief system (hell yes = go to the gym, hell no = don't go to the gym) is tough and probably feels weird.

"Not feeling like going to the gym"—or "feeling like" doing anything else important—is irrelevant. You just go anyway, and get started.

Once you get started, it's amazingly easy to finish.

And once you finish, you feel good about what you just did. And that makes it easier to go next time. And so on…

So, feel free to use the motivation tips in this document. They can give you a much-needed boost. But realize some important truths:

You won’t always feel the "hell yes"—in fact, you might feel that only rarely.

You will sometimes feel the "hell no"—in fact, a lot more often than you think you should.

If you feel "hell no" it’s OK—it's totally normal and nothing is wrong with you.

Regardless of whether you feel "hell yes" or "hell no" the only response is to go to the gym or eat the right food anyway.

Regardless of how you initially feel about it, starting the right action makes it easy to finish it.

And once you overrule your "hell no", it gets easier to do it the next time.
