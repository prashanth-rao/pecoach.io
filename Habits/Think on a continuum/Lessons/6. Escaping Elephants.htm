Pay attention to the presence of pachyderms.

Think of an elephant.

Wrinkly gray skin. Tree-stump legs. Snorty trumpet hooowaaahh sound.

The Great Escape

It's common to naturally want to avoid feeling bad. They'll do nearly anything not to feel bad. For example, trying to avoid feeling bad by... escape.

When we're confronted with things we don't like, we regress to simpler instincts.

Our thinking, planning, reflecting brain (who normally plays a mean game of chess and knows how to spell "syzygy") downshifts to "lizard brain", fight or flight mode. We don't want to think any more. We just want to find a place to hide and make ourselves feel better.

This instinct worked great when our ancestors were escaping from a tiger. See tiger, freak out, run, hide, curl up in cave... perfect.

This instinct is not so helpful when it causes us to avoid dealing with things that are healthy challenges. Things that are necessary to reaching goals.

The paradox of escape is that it makes things worse.

For example...

After you've been avoiding your boss and performance review for two weeks, not only do you still have to have that performance review, you now have to explain why you've spent the last two weeks hiding in the backseat of your car in the parking lot.

The more you avoid the actual situation, the more you think about it.

The more you try to avoid thinking about the elephant, the bigger the worry of the elephant gets. Eventually, it's elephants elephants elephants.

How are you avoiding?

Having patterns of avoiding and escaping is normal.

Those patterns could be holding you back from making the best possible progress.

How could you be avoiding your own progress?

  • Long hours at work
  • An extra drink here and there
  • A stash of treats in the house… "because the kids like them"
  • Skipping your workouts or avoiding the bits you don't like
  • Taking care of everyone else before yourself

The more these patterns are practiced, the bigger and more disruptive they can get.

Think about the elephant

Today, in order to help you do a little more, and a little better, try thinking about your elephant, whatever it is that you might be avoiding.

Use your smart, thoughtful human brain (instead of your instinctive lizard brain).

For instance:

  • Do you really need to work late on Monday, or could you get a workout in instead?
  • Could you have one less drink—or maybe a soda water?
  • Could find one healthier substitute for a treat today?
  • Could grit your teeth and do one more set, or even just one more rep, of the exercise you hate?
  • Could just say a polite "No thank you" or "Actually, that doesn't work for me" to protect time to take care of yourself?
