Learning to cook, for more spice & variety

Often, the solution to liking or being interested in something is to learn about it.

Learn to cook. Or learn to cook better.

Be adventurous in the supermarket and the kitchen.

When you know how to choose, prepare, and cook a wide variety of foods, the world is your oyster. You aren't doomed to the same few options.

You can go anywhere and do anything, confident in the knowledge that whatever green thing lands in your grocery cart or on your dinner plate... you can handle it.

Below are a few ways of preparing vegetables and maybe some novel adventures with vegetables.

Grilling and broiling

Nothing adds a wild flavor to food like grilling it over an open flame. If you don't have an outdoor grill, you can use your oven's broiler.

You can grill nearly anything. Brush the food with a little olive oil and keep a close eye on it (especially if you're broiling, because things crisp up quickly).

  • tomatoes (slice in half and grill the skin side, or broil on a cookie sheet)
  • eggplant (slice in 1"-thick slices)
  • fennel
  • peppers
  • avocado
  • asparagus
  • Portabello mushrooms
  • zucchini
  • butternut squash

Fruit works, too! Pineapple, mangoes, and apples all grill well.

For even more smoky flavor over a gas grill, place wood chips (e.g., cedar) in a small pan on the grill. Preheat the grill for a few minutes until the chips are smoking. Then add your food of choice and keep the lid closed.

Braising

Braising is cooking food that's partially covered by a little bit of liquid.

One great option for liquid is chicken or veggie stock. You can also use water, unsweetened fruit or vegetable juices (such as tomato juice), balsamic vinegar, or a little bit of dry wine or sherry (or a combination).

Braising slowly tenderizes vegetables (or meats) and adds a lot of flavor without added fat. This is particularly useful for tougher, more fibrous vegetables such as celery, carrots, beets, cabbage, and parsnips.

To braise, pour your vegetables into a pan on the stove. Add enough liquid to just barely cover them. Cook them over medium-low heat so that they simmer rather than boil.

Braising also combines well with deglazing.

Deglazing

Ever noticed that when you sauté something like meat in a pan, it leaves a bunch of browned bits behind? No frowns and steel wool.

Instead, deglaze. Keep the heat on, pour in a bit of liquid, then let the liquid simmer as you scrape up the browned bits.

Those browned bits are a result of the Maillard reaction, which happens when heat caramelizes proteins and sugars. They add the intangible "yum" factor of umami, or savoriness.

Start with sautéing some meat and/or veggies in a bit of fat such as butter or coconut oil. Cook over medium-high heat until the food browns.

Pour in some liquid, stir, and get scraping. Keep cooking until some of the water evaporates and you have a nice thick sauce.

Use fresh herbs in salad

Chop them finely and toss them in with the greens.

Use a handful or two of herbs like:

  • basil
  • oregano
  • chives
  • mint
  • sorrel
  • thyme
  • marjoram
  • chervil
  • tarragon
  • cilantro
  • dill
  • fennel tops

The brightest flavor and nutrients will be from whatever's in peak season in your area at the time.
