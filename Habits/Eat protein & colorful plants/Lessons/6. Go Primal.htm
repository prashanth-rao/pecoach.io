Ancestral dinners

It's dinner time. You're hanging out with friends and family after a nice day outside. The fresh evening air smells like wood smoke and grilled meat. The menu includes ribs, baked clams, succulent baby greens, and fresh berries with honey for dessert.

Sounds like a night at your favorite East Coast restaurant. And then... you start scratching at your fleas and smashing meat off the bone with a rock.

Tonight, you're partying like it's 19,999 BC.

This is the so-called Paleo or Primal Diet.

Idea 2: Because we've spent most of our evolution as a species consuming these particular foods, we function best when that's what we eat.

A skull found in Chad pushes the date of human origins back to 6 million years ago.

In comparison, agriculture (which eventually included breeding and growing of grains) originated about 10,000 years ago.

And high-fructose corn syrup (HFCS) has only been around since the late 1950s, while the latest artificial sweetener, Neotame, was approved by the FDA in 2002.

In other words, in human evolutionary terms, we're not used to common newfangled modern food.

Variety

We know from anthropological evidence and studies of modern-day foragers that ancestral diets vary quite a lot. When you can't drive to a grocery store, you eat whatever is around you.

For example:

  • Arctic indigenous diets are high-fat, high-protein diets of land and sea animals such as whale, seal, reindeer/caribou and fish, with few vegetables or fruits. They get their vitamins from organ meats.
  • Some indigenous African diets are mostly plant-based, high in starchy tubers and vegetables. Others are mostly animal-based, relying almost exclusively on cow's meat, milk, and blood.
  • Many indigenous diets included insects, snails, mice, snakes, and other small animals. Hey, big game hunting is tough work. Ya gotta eat something on the days you don't manage to grab a mammoth burger.

Keep it simple

Regardless of how any specific ancestral diet looked in practice, they all followed one very simple underlying principle:

Eat only whole foods that you can catch, dig up, or gather.

It's familiar advice.

Going primal

If you're interested, start thinking about some primal recipes or prep ideas that you might like to try. Or how you might adjust your daily menu to go 100% ancestral-style for a day.

You may already be well accustomed to eating ancestral-style.

For example:

Meal 1 – Eggs scrambled with vegetables and a bit of coconut oil

Meal 2 – Chicken coconut curry with vegetables

Meal 3 – Turkey meatballs + salsa with avocado

Meal 4 – Steak, salad with olive oil vinaigrette, and berries for dessert
