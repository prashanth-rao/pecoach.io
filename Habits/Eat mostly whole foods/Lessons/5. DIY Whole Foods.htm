Learn to prepare some basic recipes and have fun with whole foods.

Learning to cook (or learning to cook better) opens a wonderful world of possibilities.

People who can cook well:

  • know enough about taste and texture to have ideas on how to make creations that they enjoy;
  • are more willing to be risky and try new things;
  • understand how to put basic nutrition principles to practice, like "have lean protein" or "eat colorful vegetables";
  • aren't as intimidated by new foods or preparation methods;
  • know where to look to find help and answers for cooking problems;
  • feel confident and comfortable in the kitchen—which makes food prep a much more pleasant and fun experience.

The secret about cooking

Here is a super-secret truth about cooking: Most cooking is easy.

Seriously. Even the fancy stuff.

Yes, if you want to do Julia-Child-style authentic French cooking, it can be time-consuming and fiddly. But, every cooking task can be broken down into simple steps, and made easy. Even kids can learn to cook.

If you're a reasonably intelligent adult who has access to fire, can hold a spoon, and can follow a recipe, you can cook about 98% of anything in the world.

All learning takes trial and error. Even the best chefs have plenty of burned pots, inedible swamp mushes, or carbonized bricks in their history. There will be discarded food mistakes in the process. Don't get discouraged. Learn and keep trying.

Tools of the kitchen trade

It helps to start with a supply of some essential tools:

  • a slow cooker
  • a blender and/or food processor
  • good cookware
  • a good knife or two
  • utensils
  • a cutting board
  • food containers
  • a roasting pan or large deep casserole dish
  • a baking/cookie sheet or large oven-proof tray

They all come in very handy, to be ready when the inspiration calls.
