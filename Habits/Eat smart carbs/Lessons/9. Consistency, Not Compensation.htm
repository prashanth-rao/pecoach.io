Consistency, Not Compensation

You can't outrun a poor diet, so start with a solid foundation of smart eating choices.

Here are two important principles for success.

  • You can't out-train a poor diet.
  • Consistency beats compensation.

You can't out-train a poor diet.

(Though most of us have tried.)

No matter how hard you bust your butt in the gym or on the track, a lean body and top athletic performance comes mostly from smart food choices.

Exercising to "burn calories" or make up for "mistakes" doesn't work in the long run.

Not only does that turn movement into a chore and eating into a sin, but it eventually makes you feel run down and hungrier than ever.

Start fitness in the kitchen.

  • Choose foods that nourish you and add value to your body.
  • Eat slowly and mindfully.
  • Enjoy exercise and movement because it makes you stronger, fitter, and healthier. (Instead of trying to "work off" something "bad".) Separate movement from food, and enjoy them both for the value they offer to your body.

Focus on consistency, not compensation.

As the saying goes, an ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure.

Consistency (i.e. small daily actions you do every day) works better than compensation (i.e. frantically trying to make up for a "mistake").

Most people feel better when they focus on consistency. More in control. More relaxed. More content.

You’re also more likely to do the things you need and want to do to move towards your goals.

You’ll look for ways to just do something every day, or as regularly as possible.

For instance, your practice might be:

  • Get at least some movement today, even if that’s 5 minutes.
  • Eat at least one fruit or vegetable today.
  • Drink a big glass of water when I wake up.
  • Eat a little bit more slowly or mindfully.
  • Have a smaller portion of dessert, or skip it every other day.

And so on.

These actions might seem small and unambitious, but that’s exactly why they get done.

Things that get done, over and over, add up. They also become a seamless part of your routine, so they become easier and more automatic. Over time, these healthy habits become part of who you are, rather than an excruciating daily effort.

Doing the things leads to results. These results might not be immediate nor spectacular, but they’ll be real, and they’ll last.

Conversely, if you’re always compensating or swinging wildly between all or nothing, you’re probably spending an awful lot of time not doing any the practices that help you.

Help yourself get things done, consistently.

Anticipate. Prepare. Strategize.

When you’re done reading this, go look at your calendar.

  • What’s coming up in the next while?
  • How can you help yourself stay on track?
  • What do you need to do today . . . and tomorrow . . . and the next few days . . . to help yourself make small but wise choices, consistently?

And… how could you make movement something that you do simply because it’s good for you, and you enjoy it?
