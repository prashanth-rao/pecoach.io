Saying Difficult Things (SDT)

The power of expressing yourself.

Coach's Notes: Improving communication; asking for what one wants.

Practicing tough talk.

Asking for what you want and need and facing difficult discussions pays off in physical, emotional, and mental health.

Let's say you have an interpersonal situation you want to change.

Maybe things aren't going so well with your friend, your boss, or your spouse.

Before we can talk about how to change it, we need to know why the situation is there, and what we want as the outcome.

  • What, ultimately, do you want to happen? (Notice that this isn't about what you don't want.)
  • What, ultimately, is realistic given what you know of the situation?
  • What's important to you—what are your priorities and values?
  • What does it cost you to do nothing—to leave things as they are?
  • Why do you want to change the situation? How will change benefit you?
  • What options are there for changing it?

Being assertive

Of course, asking for what you need and want means being assertive.

Being assertive means that you interact productively with people while standing up for yourself. Being assertive doesn't mean you get whatever you want.

It means that you—and the people around you—know what you're about, and where you stand.

When you're assertive:

  • You feel good about yourself.
  • You live honestly and authentically, with integrity.
  • You make yourself mentally, emotionally, and physically healthier.
  • Other people know exactly what you stand for.
  • Other people respect you.

Assertive people:

  • express their feelings, thoughts, and desires clearly and unambiguously—with compassion, thoughtfulness, and honesty;
  • know their rights, and what they want and need; and
  • control their anger so that it's productive, and used for positive change (it's not just "letting people have it" or "saying what's on your mind").

And often, being assertive means Saying Difficult Things.

Saying Difficult Things

Many things are difficult to say.

Perhaps saying them feels shameful, awkward, or hard to articulate. People might get upset when we say them.

Saying Difficult Things (aka SDT) can be utterly terrifying.

Yet we must, at times, say these things. SDT is an act of self-preservation, self-respect, and self-care.

SDT means we have to have the courage to face someone's reaction, in order to say what must be said.

The sayer of Difficult Things must be willing to climb on that diving board, knees quaking, heart pounding.

And then, jump off anyway.

SDT is not starting an argument or "complaining". It's not "being bitchy".

SDT is the clear, explicit, direct, unambiguous articulation of your truths and values.

"It is not OK to..."

"It is not acceptable to me that..."

"Please stop..." or "Could you please do..."

"When X happens, I feel Y. And so, from now on, Z."

"I need..."

"X is bothering me because Y. I'd like to talk more about this."

"It's important to me that..."

There is tremendous power in speaking truth and defining self-protective boundaries.

It brings us more in line with our authentic selves. It gives us a chance to say who we are, what we need, and what we stand for.
