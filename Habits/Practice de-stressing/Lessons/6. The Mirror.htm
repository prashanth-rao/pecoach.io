The Mirror

How food and fitness reflect us.

How food and fitness reflect values and priorities.

Here's a simple but profound concept:

Your relationships with food, fitness, and health are the same as your relationships with your life, and with the people around you.

In other words, the health of your body is like a mirror for the rest of your life.

What does your mirror show you about your life and relationships?

What does your mirror reflect?

For example, here's what some others in coaching have said about how food and exercise mirrors their lives:

  • Food is my friend... because I feel alone.
  • Food is pleasure... because I don't have much pleasure elsewhere. (Or, I worry about being deprived… because it feels like I'm not getting enough of something elsewhere.)
  • Food has to be controlled... because my life is out of control. (Or, I lose control of food… because I spend all my energy trying to control the rest of my life.)
  • I'm afraid to ask for what I want when it comes to food... and I'm afraid to speak up in other areas of my life, too.
  • Food is meant to be shared... because I love giving to other people.
  • It's hard to take time for exercise... because I'm focused on taking care of other people, and it feels "selfish" to worry about my own needs.
  • Fitness has to be "tough" and I have to be hard on myself... because I struggle to "measure up" to other people's expectations (or my own expectations of myself).
  • I eat alone and shamefully... because I feel like I can't be open and honest elsewhere in my life.
  • I love big family meals and traditional recipes... because my family bonds are strong, and I value my culture and heritage.
  • When I eat slowly... it reminds me to "stop and smell the roses" in the rest of my life too.
  • Food is love... and sometimes I can't say "I love you" another way.

Voice of experience

Here's one story of how changing daily food practices lead to massive transitions in other relationships in life.

"Before starting coaching, my husband insisted that we already ate a 'healthy' diet, with relatively little processed food and lots of fresh vegetables. My expanding butt disagreed. And junk foods were displacing healthier choices.

It turns out that for many years, without fully recognizing it, I had been giving in to my partner's food preferences. I had made some small noises about buying organic meats and about our reliance on pasta meals. Yet mostly, I had kept my thoughts to myself.

Defining and asking for what I wanted and needed was a key moment in my coaching journey… and my marriage.

That's not to say it happened without conflict or discord. I look at it as a work-in-progress. We're doing better all the time, talking openly and compromising."
