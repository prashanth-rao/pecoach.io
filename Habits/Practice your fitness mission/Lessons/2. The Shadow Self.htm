The Shadow Self

Whatever you feed, grows. What self are you feeding?

At first glance, you wouldn't think body image expert Erin Brown has any problems.

Her glossy publicity photos show luxurious blond hair that's bouncin' and behavin'. She looks like a magnificent Goddess of Strength.

And yet...

In her blog, speaking, and books, she talks about her struggle with self-criticism and self-destruction, a cycle that started in childhood with shame over her "unlovable" body.

She hates herself.

And she loves herself.

There are two Erins inside her head.

One Erin is Beyoncé-fabulous, a funny and fierce mother bear and powerful athlete, with an infectious laugh and confident stride. The other Erin is sad, alone, scared, ashamed, and hurting.

These two women live together.

And that's OK.

Our shadow self

Our "shadow self" is the parts of ourself that we struggle most with accepting. The parts we aren't proud of. The parts we want to get away from.

Ironically, avoiding the shadow does no good.

The less aware of our shadow we are, or the more we pretend it isn't there, the stronger it gets.

Photo credit: Martin De Pasquale

It's like the monster in the closet. If we avoid opening the door and checking inside, the monster grows huge in our minds.

If we face that shadow, and open the closet, we discover that the monster is just a pile of laundry. (Sure, the socks are a little smelly, but that's the price of working out!)

Our shadow self eats shame, fear, anxiety, and avoidance. It eats "Wondering and Worrying Chow".

Conversely, our best self eats creativity, joy, fun, humor, love, and living with purpose.

Whatever we feed, grows. Which self are you feeding?

We are all only as healthy as our secrets. — Erin Brown

Practice the right path

Staying positive and focused on goals requires constant reminders.

Every day is full of temptations to make choices that are unhealthy, or disordered according to your personal goals. Living a healthy life takes a daily repeated commitment.

Feeling confusing temptations and impulses doesn't mean you've "failed" or aren't really a "fit person". It means you're human.

Every change and every goal takes daily consistent practice.

Stay on track with a clear fitness focus

Your fitness mission statement keeps you on track. It reminds you to cater to the "fit person".

Every day, you can practice reminding yourself of who you are and what you want.

Who do you want to become? Why are you doing all of this?
